STORY 1: The $150-Calorie Bar That Might Actually Be 275

What happened:
David Protein — the gold-wrapped protein bar startup from RXBar founder Peter Rahal — is facing a class action lawsuit alleging its bars contain nearly double the advertised calories (up to 275 vs. the 150 on the label) and up to 6x more fat than stated. The bars use a novel ingredient called EPG, a plant-based fat substitute that the company says passes through your body undigested, contributing only 0.7 calories per gram instead of fat's usual 9. The lawsuit says the lab tests tell a different story. Rahal calls the claims "meritless" and says the plaintiffs misunderstand how EPG works under FDA guidelines.

Why this matters:
David Protein went from zero to 3,000+ retail stores and nearly $100 million in projected revenue in under a year — one of the fastest CPG (consumer packaged goods) launches in recent memory. Rahal already proved he could build a monster brand when he started RXBar for $10,000 and sold it for $600 million. But this lawsuit hits the core of David's value proposition: the idea that you can get 28g of protein in just 150 calories. If consumers lose trust in that number, the entire brand promise crumbles. It's a reminder that in food and wellness, your nutrition label IS your marketing.

The business lesson:
**When your competitive advantage depends on a single technical claim, you're one lawsuit away from a brand crisis.** David's moat isn't taste or branding alone — it's the calorie math. That's powerful when it works, but fragile when challenged. The strongest brands build multiple layers of value so no single attack can sink them. RXBar's original pitch ("No B.S.") worked because it was a philosophy, not a specific number. David's "150 calories, 28g protein" is a spec sheet — and spec sheets can be audited.

How you could use this:
- If you're building a product, stress-test your core claim. Ask yourself: "If someone challenged this in court, would it hold up?" If your whole pitch rests on one metric (cheapest, fastest, most potent), have the receipts ready before someone demands them.
- If you're in content or marketing, notice how Rahal responded — fast, public, and with technical detail. He didn't hide. Whether or not he's right, his crisis response is a masterclass in founder-led communication during controversy.

Remember this:
A nutrition label is a promise. And broken promises don't just lose customers — they invite lawyers.

STORY 2: Your Brain Maxes Out at 3 AI Tools

What happened:
Boston Consulting Group published research in the Harvard Business Review showing that workers are hitting a cognitive wall with AI tools — a phenomenon they're calling "AI brain fry." The study of nearly 1,500 U.S. employees found that adding a second AI tool to your workflow boosts productivity, but after three tools, productivity actually drops. Workers managing lots of AI agents reported 14% more mental energy spent, 12% more fatigue, and a 39% increase in error rates. Marketing, HR, and operations teams are getting hit hardest.

Why this matters:
We're in the middle of an AI gold rush where every company is stacking tools — one for email, one for code, one for meetings, one for research. But this data suggests there's a real biological ceiling on how much AI oversight a human brain can handle. It's not unlike the tab-hoarding problem of the 2010s, but with higher stakes because these agents are actually doing things on your behalf. The emerging fix? "Agent control planes" — dashboards that let you manage multiple AI agents from one place, like an air traffic controller for your bots.

The business lesson:
**More tools doesn't mean more output — there's a point where complexity eats the productivity gains.** This is the classic "too many cooks" problem applied to AI. The companies and individuals who win won't be the ones using the most AI tools; they'll be the ones who pick the right 2-3 and go deep. Integration beats accumulation.

How you could use this:
- If you're adopting AI tools for your work or side project, resist the urge to try everything. Pick two tools max, learn them deeply, and only add a third when the first two are fully integrated into your workflow. The research literally says that's the sweet spot.
- If you're building an AI product, this is a massive opportunity. The winners in the next wave won't be "yet another AI tool" — they'll be the orchestration layer that reduces the number of tools people need to manage. Think: one ring to rule them all.

Remember this:
Two AI tools make you faster. Four make you tired. The magic number is somewhere around three — just like browser tabs should be but never are.

STORY 3: Dick's Sporting Goods and the Little League Industrial Complex

What happened:
Dick's Sporting Goods just reported record-setting Q4 sales, with the core Dick's business hitting $14.1 billion for the full year — up 5% year over year. Comparable store sales grew 4.5%. But the sleeper hit is GameChanger, the youth sports platform Dick's acquired in 2017, which has been compounding revenue at nearly 40% annually and now pulls in close to $150 million with 10 million users. Meanwhile, Dick's is rolling out "House of Sport" mega-stores (35 and counting) that feel more like community sports centers than retail shops, with batting cages, climbing walls, and turf fields inside.

Why this matters:
Youth sports in America is a $40+ billion industry, and it keeps growing because parents will spend almost anything on their kids' athletic development. Dick's figured out something clever: instead of just selling cleats and gloves, they embedded themselves into the entire youth sports experience — the scoring apps parents check obsessively, the live-streaming of Little League games, the team management tools coaches rely on. They didn't just sell to the customer; they became part of the customer's weekly routine. That's the difference between a store you visit and an ecosystem you live in.

The business lesson:
**The most durable retail businesses don't just sell products — they build platforms around the customer's lifestyle.** Dick's could've stayed a store that sells basketballs. Instead, they asked: "What does a sports parent's week actually look like?" and built tools for every part of it. GameChanger isn't a product — it's a relationship. And that relationship drives people back into Dick's stores. This is the "ecosystem lock-in" strategy that Apple perfected, applied to youth sports.

How you could use this:
- If you run any kind of business, map out your customer's full weekly routine — not just the moment they buy from you. Where else do they spend time and money? Can you build or acquire something that inserts you into those moments? A coffee shop that also hosts coworking? A tutoring service that also sells school supplies? The adjacent territory is where loyalty lives.
- Look at Dick's GameChanger acquisition: $150M in revenue from a tool they bought and grew. If you're evaluating side projects or small acquisitions, look for tools that your existing customers already use. You don't need to build a billion-dollar app — you need to own one more touchpoint in your customer's day.

Remember this:
Dick's doesn't just sell sports gear — they score your kid's Little League game. That's not a store, that's a habit.

All three stories share a common thread: the gap between what looks like the product and what's actually driving the business. David Protein's real product isn't a bar — it's a calorie claim. The lesson: in 2026, the surface-level product is just the wrapper. The real value — and the real vulnerability — lives in the layer underneath.

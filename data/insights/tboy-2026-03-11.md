STORY 1: LEGO's Best Year Ever
================================================================

WHAT HAPPENED:
LEGO reported its most profitable year ever in 2025, with revenue up 12% to DKK 83.5 billion (~$12.2B), operating profit up 18%, and net profit up 21%. They launched a record 868 SKUs — effectively 2+ new sets every single day. Consumer sales grew 16%, more than double the overall toy market growth of 7%. Top themes included City, Icons, Botanicals, Star Wars, and Technic. The company has now posted record results for three consecutive years.

WHY THIS MATTERS:
LEGO borrowed the sports content playbook — always give fans something new to talk about, collect, and debate. By launching 2 sets daily, LEGO turned its product catalog into a content calendar, keeping the conversation (and purchases) constant. While other toy companies struggle with seasonal boom-bust cycles, LEGO has created an always-on engagement machine.

THE BUSINESS LESSON:
**Turning your brand name into a verb means you've transcended the product and become the category.** "I'm LEGO-ing" isn't just wordplay — it represents a fundamental shift where the activity IS the brand. The key is relentless product cadence that creates ongoing engagement, not one-time purchases. LEGO doesn't sell toys; they sell a creative habit.

HOW YOU COULD USE THIS:
- Apply the content cadence approach to any business: small, frequent releases keep customers engaged more than big, infrequent launches. A restaurant could do a new special every 3 days. A SaaS company could ship a visible update every week. The "always something new" model works because it gives people a reason to come back.

REMEMBER THIS:
LEGO launched 2 new sets every single day and had its best year ever. Frequency beats magnitude — keep giving people a reason to come back.

STORY 2: Nvidia's 5-Layer Cake — The AI Framework
================================================================

WHAT HAPPENED:
Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang published a rare standalone blog post laying out a framework for AI as a "5-layer cake": (1) Energy at the base, (2) Chips, (3) Physical infrastructure/data centers, (4) AI models, (5) Applications on top. He argued AI isn't just software but a massive industrial buildout comparable to electrification, requiring trillions in new infrastructure. The post was timed ahead of GTC 2026 and served as both a thought leadership piece and a not-so-subtle pitch for why Nvidia's market position is durable.

WHY THIS MATTERS:
This framework reframes AI from a "tech thing" to an "infrastructure thing." If AI is a 5-layer cake, most investors and companies are only focused on layer 5 (apps). But the real bottleneck — and the real money — is in layers 1-3 (energy, chips, data centers). Huang is essentially telling the world: the AI buildout hasn't even really started yet. This is a CEO defending his company's valuation, but the framework itself is genuinely useful.

THE BUSINESS LESSON:
**When a CEO of the most important company in a megatrend tells you the real opportunity is in the unglamorous infrastructure layers, pay attention.** The picks-and-shovels play is almost always the smarter long-term bet than the gold rush itself. Everyone is building AI apps. Almost nobody is solving the energy and data center bottleneck — and that's where the real constraint (and value) lives.

HOW YOU COULD USE THIS:
- Look at any major trend and ask "what are the 5 layers?" The top layer (consumer apps) gets all the hype, but the foundational layers (energy, hardware, infrastructure) are where the durable value accrues. If you're investing, building, or hiring — look one or two layers below the hype.

REMEMBER THIS:
Jensen Huang says AI is a 5-layer cake, and everyone is fighting over the frosting while ignoring the foundation. The biggest opportunity is in the layers nobody talks about.

STORY 3: Nio's Battery Swap — Don't Charge, Just Swap
================================================================

WHAT HAPPENED:
Chinese EV maker Nio hit 100 million battery swaps and broke its single-day record with 175,976 swaps in one day. Instead of charging like Tesla, Nio drivers pull into a station and swap their depleted battery for a full one in under 5 minutes. Nio is rolling out 5th-generation swap stations in 2026 and plans to add 1,000+ new stations this year. They also offer Battery-as-a-Service (BaaS), where you buy the car without the battery and pay a monthly subscription, making the car $10K+ cheaper upfront.

WHY THIS MATTERS:
Nio's approach challenges Tesla's entire charging model. Charging takes 15-45 minutes; swapping takes 3-5 minutes. Nio also separates battery ownership from car ownership, making EVs cheaper upfront. This is a fundamentally different infrastructure bet — and it solves the single biggest complaint EV owners have: range anxiety and charging time.

THE BUSINESS LESSON:
**Sometimes the best innovation isn't a better product — it's a better delivery model.** Nio didn't build a better battery; they built a better way to deliver fresh batteries. This is the "refill, don't recharge" model. The product is the same (electricity in a battery), but the experience is radically different. Whoever solves the friction point wins, even with an inferior core product.

HOW YOU COULD USE THIS:
- Look for industries where the bottleneck isn't the product but the delivery or replenishment model. Can you "swap" instead of "charge"? Can you "subscribe" instead of "buy"? The friction point is often the real business opportunity hiding inside a product opportunity.

REMEMBER THIS:
While Tesla says "charge faster," Nio says "don't charge at all — just swap." The company that removes the wait wins.

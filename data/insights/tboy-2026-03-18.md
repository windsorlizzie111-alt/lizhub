STORY 1: Uber Eats' Spy Pricing
================================================================

WHAT HAPPENED:
Uber Eats raised marketplace fees effective March 11, 2026. The Lite tier jumped from 15% to 20%, and Plus tier restaurants now get charged an extra 5% for Uber One member orders. Custom-fee restaurants see a 3% hike capped at 30%. Beyond the restaurant fee increases, there are consumer reports and testing suggesting personalized pricing — users with Amex cards or Uber Cash credits in their accounts are seeing higher fares than identical accounts without premium payment methods. DoorDash and Grubhub have not matched these fee increases, making Uber the aggressive mover.

WHY THIS MATTERS:
Algorithmic personalized pricing is becoming more common in delivery and ride-hailing. Uber is squeezing both sides of its marketplace — restaurants pay more, and price-insensitive consumers may pay more. The "spy pricing" framing is powerful because it highlights a growing consumer concern: platforms know your spending habits, your payment method, and your order history, and they may be using that data to charge you more.

THE BUSINESS LESSON:
**Platforms with market power can extract from both supply and demand — but transparency is the ticking time bomb.** Uber can charge different prices to different people because most users don't compare prices with friends. The moment someone builds a "price comparison" tool for ride-hailing and delivery, this model breaks. The lesson for platforms: personalized pricing works until it becomes visible.

HOW YOU COULD USE THIS:
- If you're on a platform marketplace, understand how your payment method, account history, and loyalty status may affect the prices you see. Try price-checking from a logged-out browser occasionally. For business builders: personalized pricing is powerful but transparency is the risk — build it only if you can defend it when exposed.

REMEMBER THIS:
If the app knows your credit card, it might know what you're willing to pay. Uber Eats is squeezing both restaurants and rich customers — and nobody would know if they didn't compare receipts.

STORY 2: Nvidia's Coachella for Chips
================================================================

WHAT HAPPENED:
Nvidia held GTC 2026 (GPU Technology Conference) March 16-19 in San Jose — their flagship event with 1,000+ sessions, 2,000 speakers, and 450+ sponsors. Jensen Huang delivered the keynote in his signature leather jacket to a crowd of 25,000+. The conference has evolved from a niche developer event into the defining gathering for the AI industry. New product announcements, partnerships with every major cloud provider, and real-time demos of next-gen AI capabilities dominated the agenda. The podcast dubbed it "the Coachella for chips."

WHY THIS MATTERS:
GTC is no longer a product launch event — it's a cultural phenomenon that serves as the AI industry's annual gathering point. When you own the platform and the ecosystem, your conference becomes the industry's de facto home base. This creates a flywheel: the biggest announcements happen at GTC because everyone attends, and everyone attends because the biggest announcements happen at GTC.

THE BUSINESS LESSON:
**When you own the platform, your conference becomes the industry's gathering point — and that creates a flywheel of influence and deal-making.** Nvidia doesn't just sell chips at GTC. They set the agenda for the entire AI industry for the next year. The conference is a power projection tool disguised as a tech event.

HOW YOU COULD USE THIS:
- Industry events create outsized relationship and visibility returns. Whether you attend or host, being at the center of your industry's "Coachella" is a strategic asset. Even on a small scale: hosting a quarterly dinner for people in your industry can become the gathering that defines your local ecosystem.

REMEMBER THIS:
Nvidia turned a chip conference into a cultural event — and that's how you know who owns an industry. When your conference becomes the place everyone has to be, you've won more than market share.

STORY 3: The Spa/Pilates Economy
================================================================

WHAT HAPPENED:
The boutique wellness and fitness market is booming. Pilates now represents 43% of all boutique fitness studios. The global spa market is projected at $83B in 2026, growing to $244B by 2035. Pilates franchises are scaling rapidly — Pilates Addiction has sold 250+ licenses and plans to create 2,000+ jobs. The shift: consumers are moving from general-purpose gyms to specialized, premium wellness experiences. The average boutique fitness member pays $150-250/month vs. $30-50 for a gym membership.

WHY THIS MATTERS:
Wellness and fitness has shifted from gyms to premium boutique experiences. Consumers are spending 3-5x more for specialized, branded wellness vs. generic gym access. This is the same premiumization trend happening in coffee (Starbucks over drip), food (Sweetgreen over Subway), and basically every consumer category. People will pay significantly more for a curated, identity-aligned experience.

THE BUSINESS LESSON:
**The premiumization of everyday activities creates franchise-scale opportunities.** Pilates studios are the new Starbucks — premium, franchisable, and everywhere. The key insight: people don't pay extra for a "better workout." They pay extra for a branded experience that signals something about their identity. The workout is the product; the identity is the purchase.

HOW YOU COULD USE THIS:
- The "experience economy" in wellness has massive franchise potential. If you're in any service business, ask: could I specialize and charge 3x more? A generic bookkeeper charges $50/hr. A "bookkeeper for content creators" charges $150/hr for the same work, because the specialization signals expertise and belonging.

REMEMBER THIS:
Pilates studios are the new Starbucks — premium, franchisable, and everywhere. People pay 5x the price of a gym membership because the identity is worth more than the exercise.
